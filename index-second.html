Before you go, read this motivating letter.

The year was 2010.

I’d just graduated from college with $97k in student loan debt, a really bad haircut, and no choice but to move back into my parents’ basement.

If I could sum up that year of my life into one word, it’d be: frustration.

There were so many things I wanted to change. I wanted to get fit, advance my filmmaking career, and make healthier choices instead of eating cold pizza for breakfast every day.

But trying to close the gap between the person I was back then and the person I wanted to be felt paralyzing, confusing, and out of reach.

I’m willing to bet you’ve felt the same way too.

At some point, you looked around and realized you’re not living the life you envisioned for yourself.

Maybe you’re stuck repeating a cycle of behaviors that you know aren’t healthy for you, like smoking, staying up way too late, or drinking too much.

Or you realize you’ve been following other people’s rules, expectations, and versions of “success” instead of creating your own.

When this happens, we have two options.

We can continue to stay the path and hope things will get better on their own.

Or we can take the first step to creating the life we want.

For me, that first step was asking the question, “What would happen if I radically simplified my life?”

This led me down the rabbit hole of minimalism, where I met two guys crazy enough to buy into my idea for a documentary. That film ended up on Netflix and eventually led me to start my YouTube channel. And now I’m living a life that’s more aligned with my own version of success.

Of course, this didn’t happen overnight. There were a lot of baby steps, uphill battles, and failures in between…

…but none of it would have happened if I hadn’t taken responsibility for making that very first step.

So now, I want to encourage you to ask yourself the hard, but eye-opening questions:

How satisfied are you with your life?

What is *your* version of success?

What do you want your life to look like one year from now?

Tough questions, I know.

But when you take the time to pause and reflect on them, you’ll be surprised at where they take you.

To closing the gap,

Matt D’Avella
